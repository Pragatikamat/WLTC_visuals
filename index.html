VISUALISATION OF WLTC CYCLES

Worldwide harmonized Light vehicles Test Procedures (WLTP) consists of tests to measure fuel consumption, CO2 emission, pollutant emission and energy consumption values of alternative power trains.

The old lab test called the New European Driving Cycle (NEDC) was designed in the 1980s. While the NEDC test determined test values based on a theoretical driving profile, the WLTP cycle was developed using real-driving data, gathered from around the world. WLTP therefore better represents everyday driving profiles. It was developed with the aim of being used as a global test cycle across different world regions, so pollutant and CO2 emissions as well as fuel consumption values would be comparable worldwide.

The WLTP driving cycle - The Worldwide harmonized Light vehicles Test Cycles (WLTC) are chassis dynamometer tests for the determination of emissions and fuel consumption from light-duty vehicles. It is divided into four parts with different average speeds: low, medium, high and extra high. Each part contains a variety of driving phases, stops, acceleration and braking phases. For a certain car type, each powertrain configuration is tested with WLTP for the cars lightest (most economical) and heaviest (least economical) version.

The WLTC cycles vary based on vehicle categories of different power-to-mass (PMR) ratio. PMR is defined as the ratio of rated power (W) / curb mass (kg).
